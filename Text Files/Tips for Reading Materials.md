
# Tips for Reading Materials

> Welcome to the field! Balancing your core studies with supplementary content is a great strategy. Since you are in an AAS (Associate of Applied Science) program, you want resources that focus on **applied engineering**, **industry trends**, and **practical coding patterns** rather than dense academic papers or purely theoretical math.

Here is a curated list of sources that bridge the gap between "beginner lessons" and "expert research."

---

## 1. Podcasts (For Listening/Commute)

These are great for understanding how engineers think about problems without needing to stare at a screen.

### Talk Python to Me
Hosted by Michael Kennedy, this is the gold standard for Python developers. Look through his archives for episodes on "Data Science," "Automation," or "Testing." It's very accessible for first-year students and gives you a great overview of the Python ecosystem.

### Lex Fridman Podcast (Selected Episodes)
Don't try to listen to the 4-hour ones on obscure math. Look for his episodes with software engineers or AI practitioners. He does a good job of keeping the conversation grounded.

### Practical AI
This focuses on making AI "productive and accessible." It's less about how to build a model from scratch and more about how to use tools effectively—exactly what an AI Software Engineer needs to know.

---

## 2. Newsletters (For Reading/Synthesis)

Newsletters are the best way to keep up with the fast-moving AI landscape without getting overwhelmed by Twitter/X.

### TLDR AI
A daily digest of the most important AI news. It's concise and will help you build "industry literacy"—knowing which models are popular and what companies are doing.

### Python Morsels
Trey Hunner sends out very high-quality tips on Python coding patterns. It will take your basic knowledge and show you how to write "Pythonic" (clean, efficient) code.

### The Batch (by DeepLearning.AI)
Founded by Andrew Ng, this is arguably the best "middle-ground" source. It features clear, well-written summaries of recent AI developments and what they actually mean for the industry.

---

## 3. YouTube Channels (For Visual Learning)

When you want to see code in action, these channels avoid the "tutorial hell" of beginner videos:

### Corey Schafer
If you want to master Python fundamentals, his channel is legendary. He covers things like Decorators, Context Managers, and Logging—topics your classes might skim over, but that professional engineers use every day.

### Computerphile
This channel is perfect for your "level." It explains the concepts behind AI and CS (like how hashing works or how neural networks "see") using great visuals, without requiring a Ph.D. to understand.

### ArjanCodes
Once you feel comfortable writing basic Python, watch his videos on Software Design Patterns. He teaches you how to structure your code so it doesn't become a mess as you add features—a critical skill for an AI Software Engineer.

---

## 4. Communities & Practice

Instead of just reading, try "observing" how the industry works:

### Read the Docs
Pick a popular library you use (like pandas or requests) and actually read the documentation, not just the tutorials. Learning to read documentation is a "level-up" skill that separates students from engineers.

### Hacker News
Visit [ycombinator.com](http://ycombinator.com). The "Show HN" section is great for seeing what side projects other people are building. It's a great reality check to see what is possible with a few months of practice.

---

## My Recommendation for You

- **Pick one newsletter** → TLDR AI (to keep you informed)
- **Pick one podcast** → Talk Python to Me (to listen to on the go)
- **Remember:** Don't try to consume everything. AI is a massive field.
  
**Time allocation:** Spend 80% of your time on your AAS coursework and 20% on these "exploration" resources.

### Pro-tip 💡
Since you are in an AI Software Engineering program, **start learning the Command Line (CLI) and Git early**. If you can master the tools that developers use to ship code, you will be miles ahead of other students in your cohort.
